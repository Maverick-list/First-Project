Why A.I. Should Make Parents Rethink Posting Photos of Their Children Online

As generative A.I. becomes more powerful, photos shared casually on social media can be repurposed—used to train models, to build permanent profiles, or even to create harmful deepfakes. This piece explains the risks and gives practical guidance for families.


By Firza Miftahul Ilmi • Updated Sep 2025 • Investigative


Why A.I. Should Make Parents Rethink Posting Photos of Their Children Online

Introduction

Today, many parents proudly share photos of their children on social media: birthday parties, family vacations, or simple moments of joy. Yet, with the rapid advancement of technology—especially artificial intelligence (A.I.)—new challenges arise that cannot be ignored. Photos that seem “innocent” may serve as raw data in the vast and complex digital ecosystem.
Modern A.I. systems are not only capable of recognizing faces but also of processing, storing, and manipulating images in ways most parents never anticipate. From being used in massive datasets to creating deepfakes, the potential for misuse is very real. That is why parents must pause and reconsider: are the photos we upload today truly safe for the future?

What Can A.I. Do with Children’s Photos?

  1. Training Datasets Without Consent Several large A.I. datasets have included photos of children taken from public internet sources without explicit permission. For instance, a Human Rights Watch investigation revealed that images of Australian children were used in the Laion-5B dataset, which trains models like Stable Diffusion and Midjourney. The Guardian Once a photo enters a dataset, it can be copied, modified, and repurposed as generative “raw material” in ways parents cannot control.
  2. Facial Recognition and Identity Tracking A.I. can recognize facial patterns and link them with other available data—such as names, locations, or ages. Through cross-referencing, a child’s identity can be exposed.
  3. Visual Manipulation and Deepfakes With the rise of generative models, real photos can be altered into completely fabricated versions—videos, fake images, or harmful content. Having access to a child’s face increases the risk of exploitative misuse.
  4. Permanent Digital Profiling Once a photo is uploaded—on any platform—it may remain archived forever. A.I. systems can connect these images to a child’s digital identity, creating a footprint that is nearly impossible to erase.

Real Risks and Consequences for Children

The risks of sharing children’s photos online in the age of A.I. are far-reaching and long-lasting. One of the most pressing concerns is privacy over time. Photos uploaded to the internet are often permanent; even if parents decide to delete them, copies may remain stored on servers, hidden in archives, or absorbed into third-party datasets. This means that long after childhood has passed, individuals may find that personal and intimate moments from their youth are still accessible to strangers, shaping a digital identity they never consented to.

Beyond permanence, there is also the possibility of exploitation and negative representation. Children’s facial data can be weaponized, repurposed into harmful content, or manipulated through technologies like deepfakes. Such uses pose not only psychological risks but also ethical and legal challenges, as a child’s likeness may appear in contexts that are dangerous or deeply inappropriate.

Another challenge is the loss of parental control once a photo is shared publicly. What seems like a simple upload to social media may lead to images being used in ways parents never anticipated. Some platforms, or even artificial intelligence companies, can utilize these images for their own purposes—including the training of generative models—without seeking or receiving explicit permission. Experts from The Guardian have advised parents to edit photos locally before posting, limit app access to photo galleries, and, when possible, share edited screenshots instead of original files. This helps reduce the chances of sensitive images being absorbed into vast data systems.

Adding to these challenges is the uneven landscape of legal protections worldwide. Some governments have implemented strong measures to safeguard children’s digital presence. For instance, the United Kingdom has introduced the “Children’s Code,” which requires online services to apply maximum privacy settings by default for underage users. However, such protections are not universal. In many parts of the world, loopholes and inconsistent enforcement leave children’s data vulnerable to misuse, creating significant gray areas in global digital safety.

Smart Strategies for Parents in the Age of A.I.

In the face of these risks, parents are encouraged to adopt a more cautious and strategic approach when sharing family photos. Being selective is crucial: parents might avoid posting clear, front-facing images and instead choose angles from the side or back, blur identifying details, or edit photos directly on local devices before making them public. Controlling access is also essential. Sharing images through private channels such as closed family groups, while restricting social media apps from having full gallery permissions, can go a long way in preserving children’s privacy. Strong passwords, two-factor authentication, and regular privacy checks further strengthen protection.

At the same time, parents should carefully consider the platforms they use. Choosing services that are transparent about data policies and that reject the use of user content for A.I. training without permission can help reduce risks. Just as importantly, families need to build awareness and education. Parents can teach their children about the concept of digital footprints, the importance of online consent, and the long-term consequences of sharing personal images. On a broader scale, supporting stronger regulations and contributing to public awareness campaigns can help establish fairer, safer systems that protect children against the exploitative side of artificial intelligence.

Conclusion

Artificial intelligence has opened doors to incredible possibilities, but it also introduces unprecedented risks when it comes to children’s privacy. What used to be seen as private family moments now has the potential to become raw material for machine learning models and digital manipulation. With A.I. able to recognize faces, alter images, and ensure their circulation lasts indefinitely, parents can no longer think of online sharing as harmless. br
